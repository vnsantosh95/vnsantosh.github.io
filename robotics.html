1. A Rapidly Reconfigurable Collaborative Robotic Cell for Performing Satellite Assembly
(Graduate Research Assistant, Realization of Robotics System(RROS) Lab - January 2022 - August 2022)
Robots are widely used in applications which involve low mix-high volume assembly requirements. With the recent developments in collaborative robots, humans and robots can collaborate with each other and perform tasks involving high mix-low volume components. With this context, a study was made at RROS Lab, University of Southern California in partnership with a leading aerospace company to perform a collaborative assembly of a 3x scaled, open source, OreSat-0 Cube satellite. 
The project involved multiple aspects of robotics such as simulation, motion planning, computer vision, human-robot collaboration and contingency planning. My main contribution was towards the tasks categorized under computer vision and human-robot collaboration. The assembly cell involved 12 cameras for part detection and localization and 3 cameras for human tracking and gesture recognition. The part detection and localization was performed with the help of Aruco/Fiducial markers placed on the assembly table and all the components and the human tracking and gesture recognition was done by detecting the body and hand joints respectively. The vision system was developed using Python and OpenCV and the system integration with the main task planner was done using ROS in Ubuntu.
A total of 151 operations were performed to assemble 34 unique components, out of which 126(83%) were done by robots and the remaining 25(17%) were completed by a human.  The key takeaway from the project is the development and utilization of the hybrid cell(human and robot) environment for any high mix-low volume application.

2. Prototype of an Exoskeleton Arm
(Class Project - AME 341 Mechatronics - November 2022 - December 2022)
Certain applications require humans to lift heavyweight objects or perform continuous overhead work, which may lead to injuries and fatigue. To tackle this issue, exoskeleton arms and suits have been developed. But they are expensive(more than 2,000 USD) and cannot be afforded by all. A prototype of an economical(less than 500 USD) exoskeleton arm for mid-range lifting applications was developed and tested for feasibility.
The exoskeleton arm and human arm has to move synchronously and hence the design had to be same as the human arm with a joint at the elbow region. In addition, the arm had to be attached to the human arm based on its shape and accordingly a cuff shaped holder was developed. The device operates based on the mass of the object being lifted by the human arm and hence required a sensor, such as a load sensor, that could measure the necessary values. An actuator had to be used to generate torque for lifting the object and accordingly a stepper motor with a set of gears and 12 V battery were used. Finally, system integration was performed with Arduino and the device was tested for its capability. 
The prototype was built with a total cost of 150 USD and could lift 20 lbs with the actuator torque alone and total of 60 lbs along with human effort. The prototype can be scaled up with improvised design and components to develop a product less than 500 USD and lift at least 50 lbs in addition to the average mass lifted by humans.

3. Simulation - Assembly Cell for Curved Television using Webots and Dynamics and Control of 4 Legged Robots in Simulink
(Class Project - AME 547 Foundations for Manufacturing Automation - November 2021 - December 2021)
The demand for televisions in households is continuously increasing and accordingly their production has to be ramped up. Initial feasibility study was performed for curved televisions in Webots which involved physics engine to perform the simulated assembly and custom end-effectors designed in Solidworks. It was concluded that a television could be assembled with a cycle time of 4 minutes using 3 robots in collaboration with 2 humans, which translates to 60,000 units in a year at a single assembly plant. The advancement in 4 legged robots has paved way to use them in applications such as inspection, site surveying, delivery and security. To gain a basic understanding of how the robots walk, run, jump and climb in different terrains, different dynamics and controls techniques have to be studied. As a basic exercise, simulation of Unitree A1 robot using Model Predictive Control technique was performed for walking and running utilizing Matlab and Simulink.
